The world is working to address this BPE fragmentation in Abugida scripts, as it has become a significant issue affecting over a billion people. Among recent works, Velayuthan et al. (2025) proposed Grapheme Pair Encoding (GPE)\cite {velayuthan2025gpe}, which operates on Unicode grapheme clusters to avoid byte-level fragmentation. We extend the GPE into SGPE (Syllable-aware GPE) by adding a syllabification layer called LinguisTrie, which utilizes a DFA (Deterministic Finite Automaton) to ensure perfect syllable extraction before statistical compression. And Senanayake et al published an enhanced, punctuation-based Sinhala tokenizer at the 2019 National Information Technology Conference (NITC)\cite{punctuation2019tok}. Recent efforts to improve Sinhala LLMs, such as SinLlama (Aravinda et al., 2025), focused on enhancing the Llama-3-8B tokenizer with Sinhala-specific vocabulary\cite{aravinda2025sinllama}.

Historically, in the Sinhala NLP, Weerasinghe et al. (2005) have established a rule-based syllabification algorithm for Sinhala\cite{weerasinghe2005rule}. A broader record of such developments is documented in the survey by de Silva (2019) \cite{nisansa2019survey}, which includes the Sinling library\cite{sinling}, a toolkit for various Sinhala NLP tasks, including tokenization. However, these traditional methods are often hardcoded for a specific script. WWHO focuses on a script-independent approach, allowing any Abugida or complex script type to be dynamically injected, without changing the code. 

In addition to these works, Clark et al. (2022) presented a tokenization-free encoding concept, focusing on efficiency and tokenization‑free modeling\cite{clark2022canine}. A study by Dagan et al. (2024) explores several pre-tokenization approaches based on the BPE architecture\cite{dagan2024getting}. However, it was mainly focused on English, code, and general multilingual tokenization.
Among related works, Maung \& Mikami (2008) have proposed a rule-based syllabification approach that utilizes FSA (Finite State Automaton) to demonstrate the syllable
structure of Myanmar script\cite{myanmar2008fsa}. Htay et al (2008) have presented a Word Segmentation using syllable-level longest matching for the Myanmar language\cite{htay2008myanmar}.
 Although these efforts often mainly focus on ensuring the linguistic accuracy within a single language, they lack the real-world multilingual and code-switching (cross-script) tokenization required by frontier LLMs. WWHO fulfills this gap by providing a unified framework that can be integrated with existing systems, without deprecating or replacing them.
